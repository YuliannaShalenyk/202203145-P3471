Negotiating Under Fire: How Military Escalation Has Been Used to Influence Diplomatic Talks in Ukraine (2022-2026)

As world leaders push for talks, the battlefield data tells a more complicated story.

In March 2025, as US President Donald Trump spoke by phone with Russian President Vladimir Putin to discuss the possibility of ceasefire talks, the war in Ukraine did not slow down. It intensified.

Within months, civilian casualties rose sharply. By the end of the year, around 2,400 Ukrainian civilians had been killed, with nearly 12,000 injured — roughly 30 percent more than in 2024, according to an assessment seen by Bloomberg and supported by UN data. More than 2,000 of those deaths occurred after that March call.

At first glance, this looks like a contradiction. Negotiations are supposed to reduce violence, not coincide with its escalation. But over the course of the war, this pattern has appeared more than once.

Looking at data from 2022 to early 2026, a different picture begins to emerge — one where diplomacy and escalation are not separate processes, but part of the same interaction.

Across the first year of the full-scale invasion, strike numbers remained relatively limited. Monthly totals often stayed below a few hundred. But by late 2024, that had changed. The number of missile and drone attacks increased significantly, reaching several thousand per month in 2025. In some periods, totals exceeded 6,000.

What stands out is not just the scale, but the timing.

Several of the most intense spikes in attacks appear around key diplomatic moments. According to analysis by the Institute for the Study of War, Russia launched large-scale strikes involving between 400 and 700 missiles and drones in the days before or after major negotiations. These included the Alaska summit in August 2025, talks in Geneva and Moscow in November, and multiple rounds of discussions in December and early 2026.

The pattern is not perfectly consistent, but it is difficult to dismiss as coincidence. Instead, it raises the possibility that escalation itself may be part of the negotiation process.

This idea is not new. In international relations theory, coercive diplomacy describes the use of limited force to influence an opponent’s decisions. Violence, in this framework, is not only about winning on the battlefield — it is also a way of sending signals.

Some of these signals are meant to be costly. Large-scale strikes, mobilisation, and even nuclear rhetoric can serve to demonstrate commitment. The logic is simple: if a state is willing to bear high costs, it becomes more credible when it says it will not back down.

In Ukraine, this can be seen in the scale and visibility of certain attacks. Long-range missile strikes or coordinated drone assaults do more than damage infrastructure. They also communicate intent — both to Ukraine and to its international partners.

There is also an element of compellence, a concept developed by Thomas Schelling. Unlike deterrence, which aims to stop an action, compellence is about forcing change. In this case, escalation may be intended to push Ukraine toward concessions during negotiations, rather than to secure immediate military gains.

At the same time, some strikes appear to follow a different logic — one that focuses on punishment.

Throughout the war, Russia has repeatedly targeted Ukraine’s energy grid, heating systems, and water infrastructure. These attacks often intensify during the winter months, when their impact on civilians is greatest.

The winter of 2025–2026 fits this pattern. Analysts from ISW reported waves of large-scale strikes around key negotiation periods, while also noting that Russia may have been deliberately limiting the overall number of attacks. The aim, they suggest, could be to maintain pressure without undermining the appearance of engagement in peace talks.

This suggests a degree of calibration. Escalation is not constant, but adjusted — increased at some moments, restrained at others.

Yet even if escalation is being used as a form of pressure, its effectiveness remains unclear.

Since 2022, around 15,000 civilians have been killed in Ukraine, with more than 40,000 injured. Among them are hundreds of children. Despite this, the conflict has not moved closer to resolution.

One reason may be that the costs of compromise are simply too high. For Ukraine, concessions could mean accepting territorial losses or long-term insecurity. For Russia, backing down could undermine both strategic goals and political credibility.

In this situation, coercion may have limited impact. Instead of forcing agreement, it can harden positions and prolong the conflict.

Some analysts describe this as an escalation trap — a situation where attempts to apply pressure only lead to further resistance. Each side increases its efforts to compel the other, raising the overall cost of the war without changing its outcome.

Diplomatic efforts continue nonetheless. US officials have repeatedly emphasised their role in trying to bring both sides to the negotiating table.

“We are perhaps the only country in the world that can bring the two sides to discuss a potential negotiated end to the war,” US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said after recent talks.

But these negotiations are taking place under constant strain. Strikes continue before, during, and after key meetings, reinforcing the sense that diplomacy is not happening alongside the war, but within it.

Over time, a pattern becomes visible. Periods of negotiation are often accompanied by increased military activity, particularly in the form of missile and drone strikes. While this does not prove a direct causal relationship, it suggests that escalation may be used as a tool of influence, rather than simply a response to battlefield conditions.

At the same time, the limits of this approach are clear. Despite repeated attempts to apply pressure, the war continues into its fifth year, with no clear resolution in sight.

If escalation is meant to shape negotiations, it has so far failed to produce a lasting outcome. Instead, it has become part of a cycle in which diplomacy and violence reinforce each other — negotiations prompting pressure, and pressure shaping negotiations.

In Ukraine, talks have not replaced the fighting. They have happened under it.
