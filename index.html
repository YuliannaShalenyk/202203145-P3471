Escalation by the Numbers: Russia’s Winter Attacks on Ukraine’s Energy Infrastructure

From record-breaking aerial attacks to widespread outages, the winter of 2025–2026 reveals a shift toward systematic energy warfare

Imagine over a million people left without electricity, water, or heating while temperatures drop to -20°C. This is not the premise of a dystopian film, nor the result of a failed state unable to provide basic services. It is the reality unfolding in Europe’s largest country - Ukraine.

The winter of 2025-2026 became one of the harshest periods Ukrainians have endured since the start of Russia’s full-scale invasion. Unlike previous winters, this one brought no “quiet” intervals. Instead, it was marked by sustained and systematic attacks on critical infrastructure, particularly energy systems essential for civilian survival.

Over the three winter months alone, Russia carried out more than 16,800 strikes targeting civilian infrastructure. Power plants, electricity grids, and heating systems — the foundations of daily life — became primary targets. Ukrainian Prime Minister Denys Shmyhal and President Volodymyr Zelenskyy have both confirmed that there is not a single power plant in Ukraine that has not been attacked, with thousands of megawatts of generating capacity lost as a result.

This winter has been officially described as the most difficult since the invasion began, not only because of extreme weather conditions but also due to a fundamental shift in Russia’s military strategy. The intensity and scale of aerial attacks increased dramatically. In 2025 and early 2026, approximately 12,800 missiles and drones were launched — compared to just 2,800 across the entire 2022–2024 period.

February 2026 marked a peak in this escalation. Russian forces launched 288 missiles, more than doubling January’s total of 135, making it the highest monthly figure since at least early 2023. Alongside this, 5,059 long-range drones were deployed — a 13% increase from the previous month. The pace continued into March, with hundreds of drones launched in just the first week.

These figures are not simply statistics; they reflect a strategic transformation. What began as broad, retaliatory strikes has evolved into a coordinated campaign aimed at “energy isolation.” The objective is not only to damage infrastructure but to systematically dismantle Ukraine’s ability to function by fragmenting the national power grid into isolated regions unable to support one another.

These developments suggest a deliberate shift in how pressure is applied during the conflict.

By early 2026, Ukraine’s available electricity generation capacity had fallen from 33.7 gigawatts before the invasion to just 14 gigawatts. In many areas, households receive only three to four hours of electricity per day — and often at night. Russian forces frequently strike the same facilities multiple times, ensuring that damage is irreversible and repairs are delayed.

All 15 of Ukraine’s thermal power plants have now been damaged or destroyed, alongside significant damage to other forms of generation.

The humanitarian consequences of this strategy are severe. In cities reliant on district heating systems, disruptions to electricity directly translate into loss of heat and water. Hospitals, schools, and businesses are forced to operate under extreme constraints, often relying on diesel generators that cannot sustain long-term use. For many, daily life is reduced to a cycle of outages, uncertainty, and adaptation.

At the same time, the sound of air raid sirens, drones, and missile strikes has become a constant backdrop to civilian life. These attacks extend far beyond the frontlines, turning cities into targets and blurring the boundary between military and civilian spaces.

The broader goal of this strategy is clear: to make Ukrainian cities unlivable, to force population displacement, and to weaken public resilience. According to the International Organization for Migration (IOM), an estimated 325,000 Ukrainians who had previously returned may be displaced again, with more than one-third considering leaving the country once more.

Yet, this pressure has also accelerated a shift in Ukraine’s energy strategy. The vulnerabilities of a centralized energy system — reliant on large power plants and interconnected grids — have become increasingly apparent. In response, Ukraine has begun transitioning toward a more decentralized model.

This includes the installation of small modular gas turbines, rooftop solar systems, and localized energy storage solutions. Unlike large power plants, these smaller systems are harder to target and can continue operating independently if parts of the grid are compromised. Municipal cogeneration units, capable of producing both electricity and heat, have also been deployed to reduce reliance on centralized infrastructure.

This transition is not only technological but strategic.

On March 3, President Zelenskyy chaired a National Security and Defense Council meeting that approved regional energy resilience plans for every part of the country. These plans focus on four key areas: protection of critical infrastructure, expansion of decentralized energy capacity, development of independent heating systems, and strengthening water supply resilience.

While winter has passed, the attacks have not. Civilian casualties continue to rise, with dozens killed or injured daily far from the frontline. These are not battlefield losses, but the human cost of a strategy that targets the systems people rely on to live.

At the same time, Ukraine continues to appeal to its international partners for support — not only to rebuild what has been destroyed, but to protect what remains. The past winter has made one thing clear: energy infrastructure is no longer a secondary casualty of war, but one of its central battlegrounds.

For millions of Ukrainians, survival is no longer defined solely by proximity to the frontlines, but by access to heat, light, and water. As the war stretches into another year, the question is not only how long the fighting will last, but how long civilian systems can withstand this sustained pressure — and what it will take to keep them running.
